Role play activities

Practise real placement interactions across three levels of complexity

Your role: Take a passive, observational stance. Your goal is to notice what's happening and consider what you might do differently as the teacher.
Year 7 Maths · Silent Student

The Disengaged Learner

You are teaching a Year 7 maths class on fractions. One student sits quietly at the back, head down, not responding to instructions. They don't answer questions, don't open their book, and make no eye contact. The rest of the class is working.

Observe and consider: What non-verbal cues might tell you more? What would you do first — approach the student, speak to the class, or continue teaching? What would a brief, private check-in sound like?
Year 9 English · Off-Task Student

The Phone Under the Desk

You are teaching a Year 9 English class. A student has their phone under the desk and is clearly texting. They laugh occasionally and show another student their screen. The school has a no-phone policy.

Observe and consider: How do you address this without escalating? What's the school's procedure? How do you handle it privately versus in front of the class? What language would you use?
Year 5 Science · Student in Distress

Something's Wrong at Home

A usually engaged Year 5 student arrives to science visibly upset. They sit down but don't get out their equipment. When you begin the lesson, they put their head on the desk. Two students nearby exchange concerned looks.

Observe and consider: What are the welfare indicators here? How do you balance the lesson with checking in? What would you say and when? Who else needs to know?
Year 10 Humanities · Challenging Authority

The Student Who Questions You

You ask the class to complete a task. One student says loudly: "Why do we even have to do this? It's pointless." Several students turn to see your reaction. The comment isn't aggressive, but it's clearly a test of your authority.

Observe and consider: How do you validate the question without undermining your authority? What tone do you use? How do you return the focus to learning quickly?
Your role: Take an active supporting role. You'll practise intervening, redirecting, and navigating trickier situations with students.
Year 8 Maths · Blank Worksheet

I Don't Get It

A student has a completely blank worksheet halfway through the lesson. When you crouch next to them and ask what's happening, they say: "I don't get any of it. I've never understood fractions." They look defeated, not defiant.

Your role: Practise a brief, supportive check-in. Break down the task to its simplest starting point. Use one concrete example. Ask what part makes sense to them. Avoid doing the task for them.
Year 9 English · Task Refusal

I'm Not Doing It

You ask the class to begin a persuasive writing task. One student says flatly: "I'm not writing that. It's dumb." They cross their arms. The class watches. It is not aggressive — but it is a direct refusal.

Your role: Practise a low-key, private redirect. Offer the student a way to engage without backing down from the task. Avoid a public standoff. Use a quiet, calm tone and minimal words.
Year 7 Science · Disruptive Pair

The Chatty Two

Two Year 7 students are talking throughout your instructions. You've made eye contact with them twice. They've briefly quieted each time, then resumed. The rest of the class is becoming distracted.

Your role: Practise moving into proximity without stopping your instruction. Then, if needed, a quiet private redirect. Then, if needed, a consequence — without escalation or emotion.
Year 6 Maths · Early Finisher

Done. Now What?

A high-achieving student finishes the task in 8 minutes. They look up at you: "I'm done. Can I go on my computer?" You haven't planned a specific extension activity for this lesson.

Your role: Practise a quick verbal extension prompt that deepens thinking rather than adding more of the same. Try: "Can you explain why your method works? Is there another way?" or "Write me a question that would be harder than this one."
Your role: Take full responsibility for whole-class leadership. Practise your presence, your transitions, and your key teaching moves.
Any Year Level · Lesson Opener

The First 3 Minutes

You are starting a new lesson. Students are still settling — chairs scraping, bags being put away, a few conversations continuing. You are at the front. You have not yet spoken.

Your role: Practise signalling the start of the lesson without shouting. Use silence, presence, and a visible cue (e.g. countdown on the board, a quiet task already displayed). Goal: all students ready within 3 minutes, without raised voices.
Any Year Level · Questioning

Beyond "Any Questions?"

You have just delivered a 10-minute explanation. You want to check understanding before releasing students to independent work. "Does everyone understand?" gets a few nods and silence.

Your role: Practise at least three different questioning techniques: cold call (by name), turn and talk, mini-whiteboard responses, or an exit slip prompt. Aim for all students responding, not just the confident ones.
Any Year Level · Reading the Room

They've Lost Interest

You are 25 minutes into a 50-minute lesson. You can see that energy is dropping — students are fidgeting, two have their heads down, the pace of work has slowed significantly. You still have the main task to complete.

Your role: Practise an in-the-moment re-engagement move. A brief brain break, a change of task format (pair share instead of individual writing), or a surprising question. The move should take under 2 minutes.
Year 10 · Difficult Discussion

The Sensitive Topic

During a Year 10 English discussion on a text dealing with domestic violence, a student makes an insensitive comment. The room goes quiet. Two other students look visibly uncomfortable.

Your role: Practise a calm, brief, non-punitive correction that re-centres the discussion on respectful analysis. Then re-launch the discussion. Follow up with affected students after class.
